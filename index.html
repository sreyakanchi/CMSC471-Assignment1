Visualization write-up

Dataset

This visualization draws on the Urban Displacement Project's gentrification dataset, covering 2,799 census tracts across five U.S. cities: New York City, Baltimore, Washington D.C., Atlanta, and Oakland. Each row represents one census tract — a small, relatively stable geographic subdivision of a county used by the U.S. Census Bureau, typically housing 1,200–8,000 people. The dataset compares 2000 Census figures to 2013–2017 American Community Survey 5-year estimates.

Data transformations

Two key transformations were applied. First, racial group counts were converted to percentage shares of each tract's total population for both years, and a change score (2017 share minus 2000 share, in percentage points) was computed for each group. This allows fair comparison across tracts of different sizes. Second, medians — rather than means — were used as the summary statistic across tracts. Median is more robust to outlier tracts with extreme demographic shifts, which is important in a dataset where individual tracts can swing dramatically.

Design choices

The primary chart uses a grouped bar chart to show racial composition change side-by-side for gentrified and non-gentrified tracts. A grouped bar was chosen over a stacked bar because the goal is comparison between groups (gentrified vs. not), not part-to-whole composition. The y-axis includes zero and spans both positive and negative values, so the direction of change is immediately visible without distortion. Blue encodes gentrified tracts throughout all four charts for consistency; gray encodes non-gentrified tracts. These colors were chosen to be perceptually distinct without implying a value judgment (e.g., red/green, which carry good/bad connotations).

The economic charts (income and home value) deliberately start at $0 on the y-axis to avoid truncation bias. The education chart uses a horizontal comparison of just two bars — a simple encoding appropriate for a simple two-group comparison.

All chart titles are descriptive and neutral. The annotation beneath the main chart explicitly states the key finding in plain language and includes the caveat that medians may understate individual tract displacement. The methodology box discloses how "gentrified" is operationally defined, including that educational attainment change is one of the two classification criteria — which is directly relevant to interpreting the education chart.

Ensuring clarity and accuracy

Every axis is labeled with units. Legends identify which color represents which group and include sample sizes (n = 289, n = 2,510) so readers can assess the relative weight of each category. The header sub-title discloses the time period and data sources. All four racial/ethnic groups present in the data are shown — no group is omitted to strengthen a narrative. The methodology and limitations sections below the charts disclose the definition of gentrification used, the geographic scope of the dataset, and the measurement limitations of comparing Census 2000 to ACS 2013–2017 estimates.

Methodology & data transparency

How "gentrified" is defined in this dataset

A census tract is classified as eligible for gentrification if its median household income was below the 40th percentile of its metro area in 2000 and its median home value was in the bottom 40th percentile. Of eligible tracts, a tract is classified as gentrified if it experienced both:

  • Above-median home value growth between 2000 and 2017 (top half of eligible tracts by % change)
  • Above 66th percentile increase in the share of adults with a bachelor's degree or higher

This definition follows the Urban Displacement Project framework. Alternative definitions exist in gentrification literature — results may differ. Tracts flagged as low-population were excluded.

Limitations & potential bias

Coverage is limited to five cities; NYC alone makes up 77% of tracts, so findings should not be generalized broadly. Tract-level composition change does not directly measure individual displacement — a rising white share could reflect departures, arrivals, or both. Racial figures reflect self-identification and minor measurement differences may exist between Census 2000 and ACS 2013–2017.
