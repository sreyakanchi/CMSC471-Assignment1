Mobility Gaps

How does race and gender shape individual income rank and employment at age 27 — controlling for where parents started on the income ladder?

Data: Opportunity Insights
Raj Chetty et al., National Cohort Study
Birth cohorts 1978–1992

Individual Income Rank (KIR)

Mean percentile rank in national income distribution at age 27.

Employment Rate (EMP)

Fraction of children employed at age 27, by race and gender across birth cohorts.

This visualization is based on data from a study conducted by Raj Chetty's team, "Changing Opportunity: Sociological Mechanisms Underlying Growing Class Gaps and Shrinking Race Gaps in Economic Mobility." We specifically looked at the subcategory, “National-Level Outcomes by Birth Cohort, Parental Income, Race, and Gender” (linked here) which analyzes data from 57 million children, examining economic mobility across birth cohorts in the US. The variables we chose to focus on were race/ethnicity, gender, and parent income, and their effects on measures of economic status – individual income rank (KIR) and employment rate – over time.

We implemented various filters for the data to help users see our variables’ direct impact on economic status. We used buttons for the race/ethnicity filters to make it intuitive to toggle visibility, along using distinct colors for each of the groups. We initially used buttons for gender and parent income, but later switched them out for more intuitive controls. Gender is represented by a dropdown menu, which by default shows the combined data and can isolate either gender. We initially displayed all gender categories separately, resulting in ten lines per graph, but later combined them to reduce visual clutter. Parent income percentile is represented by a slider from 1 to 100%, with breakpoints every 25%.

The two graphs in the visualization both follow measures of economic mobility as the y axis and use birth cohort year as an x axis. We considered using a bar chart so we could compare race and economic status more directly, but settled for using a line chart to also document trends with birth year and show how impacts on social mobility over time. As a user controls the variables, sliding transitions in the graph help them see significant changes.

While making this visualization, we had a couple of important findings. Firstly, there is a consistent inequality when it comes to individual income ranks (KIR) for the different races, which has not changed over the years. As parent income increases, we found that the KIR of white men and women significantly jumps, especially compared to those of other groups. We also noticed that while the employment rate also increased as parent income did, it increased more significantly for white people over the years. These two findings may suggest a systematic problem when it comes to social mobility based on race and its consistency between years suggests that this is not being solved. Another finding we noticed was that gender disparities were relatively minimal, but there was a single exception of black men having around 8-9% lower employment rates when compared to black women, which may suggest a problem of oppression of black men in the workforce.
